第五章：学会学习 / Learning to Learn

核心问题：AI 时代的自我调节学习策略 / Self-regulated learning strategies for the AI era

5.1 开篇故事：两位开发者，两条路径

同一天入职，同一间办公室。开发者小张和开发者小李站在同一条起跑线上。

小张是「AI 全开」型。写代码？让 Copilot 生成。遇到 bug？把错误信息丢给 ChatGPT，三分钟拿到修复方案。写文档、写测试用例、查 API 文档——凡是能交给 AI 的，他绝不亲自动手。六个月后，小张的交付效率是团队平均水平的整整三倍。领导在周会上点名表扬，同事纷纷来请教他的「AI 工作流」。

小李则截然不同。他也会用 AI，但用得小心翼翼。遇到问题，他先自己琢磨半小时，实在卡住了才去 AI 那里要个「提示」而不是答案。他让 AI 解释概念，却坚持自己推导一遍。他常常失败——一个简单功能，小张用 AI 半小时搞定，小李可能要折腾一整天。同事私下议论：小李是不是太固执了？

但在第十二个月，形势逆转了。

公司接了一个前所未有的项目：技术栈是全新的，需求模糊，没有现成的参考实现。小张打开 ChatGPT，输入问题，得到的回答要么过于泛化，要么根本不适用。他习惯了「提问—获得答案」的节奏，却不知道如何在没有现成答案时自己搭建思维框架。他反复追问 AI，得到的只是一堆零散的碎片，他无法将它们整合成可执行的方案。

小李则开始崭露头角。过去一年里，他每一次「费力」的挣扎，都在脑海中建构了心智模型（mental model）。他知道如何拆解陌生问题、如何假设、如何验证、如何迭代。当 AI 无法直接给出答案时，他能够自己搭建脚手架，然后再用 AI 来填补细节。他解决的是新问题（novel problems）——那种 AI 训练数据里从未出现过的情境。

小张会用工具，却离不开工具。小李建构了可迁移的心智模型，在工具失效时仍能思考。

这不只是程序员的困境。柏林一位钢琴教师发现，学生们开始用 AI 生成的指法建议来「跳过」基础练习。短期内他们能弹出更复杂的曲子，但在即兴演奏和视奏（sight-reading）环节——需要深层肌肉记忆和直觉判断的场景——他们的表现明显退化。芬兰一家工程咨询公司的培训主管也观察到类似模式：年轻工程师用 AI 跳过了手算结构力学的阶段，在面对非标准项目时缺乏「结构直觉」。学习的捷径，有时恰恰是成长的弯路。

5.2 核心命题：学习不等于表现

学习（learning）和表现（performance）是两回事。这一区分在 AI 时代变得至关重要。

AI 能瞬间提升你的表现。你问，它答；你写不出代码，它写；你不知道某个概念，它解释。在「完成任务」的维度上，AI 是超级加速器。但表现提升不等于学习发生。你在考试前背下的公式，考完就忘；你让 AI 写出的代码，下次遇到类似问题时，你依然写不出。表现是即时的、可测量的产出；学习是内化的、可迁移的能力。

认知科学中有一个反直觉的结论：学习过程中的困难（difficulty during learning），往往能带来更好的长期记忆和迁移。Bjork（1994）称之为合意困难（desirable difficulties）——刻意制造的学习难度，反而促进深度内化。分散练习（spacing）、交错练习（interleaving）、自我测验（testing）——这些方法之所以有效，正是因为它们让学习「变难」了。而 AI 的捷径，往往消除的正是这些困难，顺带也消除了学习本身。

学习情境中的元认知（metacognition in learning），意味着知道何时使用 AI 辅助、何时刻意不用。这不是对 AI 的排斥，而是对学习目标的清醒判断：此刻，我是在「产出」还是在「成长」？如果答案是后者，也许我该先独自挣扎一会儿。

5.3 证据与思想实验

5.3.1 布鲁姆认知目标分类遇上 AI

修订版布鲁姆认知目标分类（Bloom's Taxonomy, 2001）将认知过程分为六个层次：记忆（Remember）→ 理解（Understand）→ 应用（Apply）→ 分析（Analyze）→ 评价（Evaluate）→ 创造（Create）。从低到高，每一层都建立在前一层之上。

AI 在前三层表现得相当出色。记忆：它可以替你存储和检索几乎任何信息。理解：它可以解释概念、概括（summarise）文档、建立联系。应用：它可以生成代码、起草报告、执行标准流程。在分析层，AI 也在快速进步——它能拆解论证、识别模式、比较方案。但在评价和创造两层，人类依然占据主导。评价涉及价值判断、优劣权衡、情境敏感度；创造涉及真正的原创、对既有范式的突破、对「未曾存在过的东西」的想象。AI 可以辅助这两层，却难以独立完成。

元认知的问题因此变成：此刻，我处于哪个认知层次？我需要向更高层迈进吗？ 如果你的工作始终停留在前三层，AI 会越来越能替代你。如果你能持续操练评价与创造，你就是在建构 AI 难以复制的护城河。

5.3.2 合意困难：为什么「难学」反而学得好

Bjork（1994）在《记忆与元记忆》中系统阐述了合意困难（desirable difficulties）的悖论：让学习变难，反而能提高长期学习效果。

三种典型的合意困难是：分散练习（spacing）——把学习分散到多天而非一次性 cram；交错练习（interleaving）——交替练习不同类型的问题，而非集中练一种；检索练习（retrieval practice）——通过测验自己来强化记忆，而非反复阅读。每一种都会在当下制造「困难」——你记不住、混淆、答错——但从长远看，这些困难促进了更深层的编码和更强的迁移能力。

AI 的捷径恰好消除了这些困难。你不需要记忆——问 AI 就行。你不需要检索——AI 帮你查。你不需要在多种类型间切换——AI 直接给你针对当前问题的答案。便捷性换来了即时表现，却牺牲了长期学习。

这并非呼吁你放弃 AI。而是提醒：当你处于「学习模式」时，要有意识地保留一定程度的困难。例如，在让 AI 给出完整解答之前，先尝试自己推导几步；在查 AI 文档之前，先尝试回忆；在完成一个类型的问题后，主动切换另一类型，而非让 AI 替你批量处理同类任务。

5.3.3 刻意练习与 AI 辅助捷径

Ericsson 与 Pool（2016）在《刻意练习》（Peak）中提出：刻意练习（deliberate practice）的核心在于在能力边缘操作，并获得即时反馈。你要不断地把自己推到「刚刚超出舒适区」的位置，在那里失败、调整、再试。反馈告诉你错在哪里，下一次迭代让你更靠近目标。

AI 能提供反馈——它可以把你的代码、你的论证、你的写作拿去分析，指出问题。但如果 AI 同时也替你做了工作，你就没有「边缘」可以推了。你失去了「费力尝试—失败—调整」的循环。没有这个循环，就没有刻意练习，也就没有真正的技能建构。

类比：健身。教练可以给你反馈（姿势对不对、重量合不合适），但如果教练替你举哑铃，你的肌肉不会增长。AI 可以是你的教练，但不能替你举哑铃。

5.3.4 AI 导师的两难：给答案 vs. 苏格拉底式追问

当 AI 扮演导师角色时，存在一个根本性的张力：直接给答案能快速解决问题，但可能削弱学习；苏格拉底式追问（Socratic questioning）——通过提问引导学习者自己发现——能促进深度理解，但更耗时，且对 AI 的提示设计要求更高。

Dong 等人（2025）在《认知之镜》（The Cognitive Mirror）中提出了一种有趣的思路：让 AI 扮演「可被教的初学者」，而非全知全能的先知。学习者向 AI 解释概念，AI 扮演不理解的学生，提出疑惑、请求澄清。这种「教别人」的情境，迫使学习者主动组织知识、检测自己的理解漏洞——正是费曼技巧（Feynman technique）的数字化实现。

元认知的启示：当你用 AI 学习时，可以主动选择模式。需要快速产出时，直接要答案无妨。需要真正内化时，尝试让 AI 扮演追问者或初学者，逼自己把理解说清楚。

5.3.5 思想实验：自行车的类比

AI 好比电动自行车。它可以让你更快到达目的地，省力、高效。但如果你从未踩过踏板，你的腿部肌肉会萎缩。当电池没电时——新奇问题、缺乏数据、前所未见的情境——你还能骑车吗？

许多知识工作者正在经历「电动自行车效应」：他们用 AI 完成日常任务，效率惊人。但当遇到 AI 无法直接回答的问题、当需要在不完整的信号下做判断、当必须创造从未存在过的解决方案时，他们发现自己「不会骑了」。他们依赖 AI 的输出，却没有建构独立骑行的能力。

元认知问题：你是在用 AI 增强你的骑行能力，还是在用 AI 替代骑行本身？前者是杠杆；后者是拐杖。拐杖在平坦的路上好用，在没路的地方就失效了。

5.3.6 自我调节学习循环：齐默尔曼模型

Zimmerman（2002）提出的自我调节学习（self-regulated learning）包含三个相位：预思（forethought）——设定目标、制定计划、激活动机；执行（performance）——实施学习活动、监控进程、运用策略；反思（self-reflection）——评估结果、归因分析、调整下一次策略。

AI 可以在每个阶段提供支持。预思时，AI 能帮你拆解目标、生成学习计划。执行时，AI 能提供即时答案、解释、练习。反思时，AI 能帮你总结、对比、发现薄弱点。但前提是：学习者保持元认知控制。如果学习者把控制权完全交给 AI——让 AI 决定学什么、什么时候学、学得怎么样——那么 AI 就从脚手架（scaffold）变成了拐杖（crutch）。脚手架会逐渐（gradually）撤离，帮助你独立；拐杖会让人越用越依赖。

目标与计划] --> B[执行
学习与监控] B --> C[反思
评估与调整] C --> A end

5.3.7 何时用 AI 当导师，何时独自挣扎：决策框架

第四章讨论了注意力分配的四象限（创造、评估、消费、反应）。学习模式与生产模式的切换，本质上也是一种注意力分配决策：你是把注意力投向「产出结果」还是「构建能力」？这两者的权衡，需要元认知觉察。

一个实用的元认知决策框架，可以基于三个问题：

  1. 这是我需要内化的技能吗？ 如果这是你核心能力的一部分，值得长期持有，那么多花时间挣扎是投资。如果这是一次性任务、边缘技能，用 AI 加速是理性选择。

  2. 我此刻处于学习模式还是产出模式？ 学习模式下，目标是「我会了」；产出模式下，目标是「完成了」。两者可以交替，但要清醒——不要骗自己说「我在学习」却一直在要现成答案。

  3. 这里的 AI 辅助会建构还是侵蚀我的能力？ 如果 AI 帮你跨越了「费力尝试」的环节，直接给答案，你可能会失去建构心智模型的机会。如果 AI 提供的是反馈、提示、苏格拉底式追问，则可能在建构能力。

5.4 反面论证：并非所有挣扎都有益

并非所有困难都是「合意」的。有时，最快路径就是正确路径。

AI 辅助可以释放认知资源，用于更高层次的学习。如果你用 AI 处理重复性任务——格式转换、数据整理、模板填充——你把省下的脑力用于真正的创造性思考或深度理解，那是双赢。问题不在于「用不用 AI」，而在于意向性（intentionality）：你是有意识地选择用 AI 走捷径，还是默认地依赖它？

此外，某些类型的「挣扎」纯粹是浪费时间。卡在一个 typo 上两小时、对着过时的文档反复试错、在已有成熟解法的问题上重新发明轮子——这些不是合意困难，而是无效消耗。元认知的价值在于区分：哪些困难值得保留，哪些该用工具绕过。

5.5 元认知时刻：三个反思练习

练习一：学习 vs. 表现审计（The Learning vs. Performance Audit）

列出你最近五次使用 AI 的情境。可以是写代码、写邮件、查资料、做分析——任何一次。

对每一次，问自己三个问题：

  1. 我当时处于学习模式还是产出模式？ 我的主要目标是「学会」还是「完成」？
  2. 我是有意识地做出这个选择的吗？ 还是习惯性地打开了 AI？
  3. 如果重来一次，我会做出不同的选择吗？ 为什么？

许多人会发现：他们很少有意识地区分（explicitly distinguish）这两种模式。他们默认用 AI，因为「更快」。审计的目的，是让这种默认变得可见，从而恢复选择权。

练习二：挣扎预算（The Struggle Budget）

为接下来的一周做一份「挣扎预算」。刻意分配：什么时候你会主动选择不用 AI，独自挣扎？什么时候你会用 AI 来提升产出？

写下来。例如：「周二上午，学习新框架的 API 设计，前两小时不问 AI，先自己读文档和尝试。」「周三下午，写项目周报，用 AI 生成初稿。」关键不是比例——有人可能 80% 用 AI，有人 20%——而是意向性。你是在按计划分配，而非被习惯推着走。

练习三：迁移测试（The Transfer Test）

回想一件你上个月「用 AI 学过」的东西。可以是一个技术概念、一个工具用法、一个解决问题的思路。

问自己：我能从零开始解释它吗？ 不查 AI，不翻记录，用白纸或白板，从头讲给别人听。如果能，说明学习发生了。我能在新情境中应用它吗？ 换一个类似但不完全相同的场景，你能迁移过去吗？

如果两者都做不到，也许你当时得到的只是「表现」，而非「学习」。这不是失败——而是下一次学习时，你可以有意识地保留更多困难，让真正的内化发生。

5.6 总结与过渡

本章要点

  • 学习不等于表现：AI 能提升即时表现，但学习需要合意困难与生产性挣扎（productive struggle）。
  • 布鲁姆分类与 AI 能力：AI 胜任记忆/理解/应用，人类在评价/创造上仍居主导；元认知问题是「我该向更高层迈进吗？」。
  • 合意困难：分散、交错、检索练习——让学习变难反而促进长期效果；AI 捷径消除困难，也消除学习。
  • 刻意练习：在能力边缘操作才有成长；AI 可提供反馈，但不能替你「举哑铃」。
  • 自我调节学习：预思—执行—反思的循环中，保持元认知控制，让 AI 作脚手架而非拐杖。
  • 决策框架：需内化吗？学习模式还是产出模式？AI 会建构还是侵蚀能力？

从「学会学习」（本章）出发，我们即将面对元认知中最艰难的一环：价值判断。知道该学什么、怎么学，终究要落到「什么值得做」「什么是对的」——这不是认知能力，而是伦理与价值观的领地。下一章，我们将探讨元认知与价值判断——在 AI 能产出一切的时代，人类如何做出真正属于自己的选择。

Chapter 5: Learning to Learn

Core Question: Self-regulated learning strategies for the AI era

5.1 Opening Story: Two Developers, Two Paths

Same hire date, same office. Developers Alex and Ben stood at the same starting line.

Alex was "AI all-in." Code? Let Copilot generate it. Bug? Paste the error into ChatGPT, get a fix in three minutes. Documentation, test cases, API lookups—anything AI could handle, he outsourced. Six months later, Alex shipped features at three times the team average. Leadership praised him in all-hands; colleagues asked for his "AI workflow."

Ben was different. He used AI too, but cautiously. When stuck, he wrestled on his own for half an hour before asking AI for a "hint," not an answer. He asked AI to explain concepts, then insisted on deriving them himself. He often failed—a simple feature that took Alex half an hour with AI might take Ben all day. Colleagues whispered: Was Ben just too stubborn?

By month twelve, the tables turned.

The company landed an unprecedented project: a new tech stack, fuzzy requirements, no ready reference implementation. Alex opened ChatGPT and typed his questions. The answers were either too generic or plainly wrong. He had grown used to "ask—get answer"; he didn't know how to build a thinking framework when no ready answer existed. He kept prompting, but got only scattered fragments he couldn't turn into executable plans.

Ben started to shine. Every "effortful" struggle over the past year had built mental models (心智模型) in his mind. He knew how to break down unfamiliar problems, how to hypothesize, verify, and iterate. When AI couldn't give direct answers, he could build his own scaffold and then use AI to fill in details. He solved novel problems (新问题)—situations that never appeared in AI training data.

Alex could use tools but couldn't think without them. Ben had built transferable mental models and could still think when tools failed.

This isn't just a programmer's dilemma. A piano teacher in Berlin noticed students using AI-generated fingering suggestions to "skip" fundamental exercises. Short-term, they played more complex pieces, but in improvisation and sight-reading—scenarios requiring deep muscle memory and intuitive judgment—their performance visibly deteriorated. A training director at a Finnish engineering consultancy observed the same pattern: junior engineers who used AI to skip hand-calculating structural mechanics lacked "structural intuition" when facing non-standard projects. The shortcut to learning is sometimes the detour from growth.

5.2 Core Thesis: Learning Is Not Performance

Learning (学习) and performance (表现) are not the same. This distinction matters more than ever in the AI era.

AI can instantly boost performance. You ask, it answers; you can't write code, it writes; you don't know a concept, it explains. In the "get it done" dimension, AI is a super-accelerator. But improved performance does not mean learning has occurred. Formulas you cram before an exam vanish afterward; code AI wrote for you—you still can't write it the next time. Performance is immediate, measurable output; learning is internalized, transferable capability.

Cognitive science offers a counterintuitive conclusion: difficulty during learning (学习中的困难) often leads to better long-term retention and transfer. Bjork (1994) calls this desirable difficulties (合意困难)—deliberately creating difficulty in learning actually promotes deeper encoding. Spacing (分散练习), interleaving (交错练习), retrieval practice (检索练习)—these work precisely because they make learning harder. AI shortcuts remove exactly those difficulties, and with them, learning itself.

Metacognition in learning (学习情境中的元认知) means knowing when to use AI assistance and when to deliberately withhold it. This is not rejection of AI but a clear judgment of learning goals: Am I in "output" mode or "growth" mode? If the latter, perhaps I should struggle alone a little first.

5.3 Evidence and Thought Experiments

5.3.1 Bloom's Taxonomy Meets AI

The revised Bloom's Taxonomy (布鲁姆认知目标分类, 2001) divides cognitive processes into six levels: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create. Each level builds on the previous.

AI performs well on the first three. Remember: it can store and retrieve almost any information. Understand: it can explain concepts, summarize (概括) documents, and draw connections. Apply: it can generate code, draft reports, and execute standard flows. At Analyze, AI is improving—it can decompose arguments, spot patterns, compare options. But at Evaluate and Create, humans still lead. Evaluation involves value judgment, trade-offs, context sensitivity; creation involves true originality, breaking existing paradigms, imagining what has never existed. AI can assist but struggles to do these alone.

The metacognitive question thus becomes: At which level am I operating? Do I need to go higher? If your work stays in the first three levels, AI will increasingly replace you. If you keep exercising evaluation and creation, you build a moat AI cannot easily copy.

AI competent] --> B[Analyze
AI improving] B --> C[Evaluate/Create
Human led] end

5.3.2 Desirable Difficulties: Why Harder Learning Works Better

In "Memory and Metamemory" (1994), Bjork spells out the paradox of desirable difficulties (合意困难): making learning harder improves long-term outcomes.

Three classic desirable difficulties: spacing—spreading learning across days rather than cramming; interleaving—mixing different problem types instead of drilling one kind; retrieval practice—testing yourself to reinforce memory rather than rereading. Each creates immediate difficulty—you forget, confuse, get it wrong—but over time, these difficulties promote deeper encoding and stronger transfer.

AI shortcuts remove those difficulties. You don't need to remember—ask AI. You don't need to retrieve—AI looks it up. You don't need to switch types—AI gives you answers tailored to the current problem. Convenience trades away long-term learning for immediate performance.

This is not a call to abandon AI. It's a reminder: when in "learning mode," deliberately preserve some difficulty. Try deriving steps before asking AI for full solutions; try recalling before consulting AI docs; switch problem types yourself rather than having AI batch-process them.

5.3.3 Deliberate Practice vs. AI-Assisted Shortcuts

In Peak (2016), Ericsson and Pool argue that deliberate practice (刻意练习) requires operating at the edge of your ability with immediate feedback. You keep pushing yourself to "just beyond comfort," where you fail, adjust, and try again. Feedback shows where you went wrong; the next iteration moves you closer.

AI can provide feedback—it can analyze your code, your argument, your writing and point out issues. But if AI also does the work, there is no edge to push against. You lose the "effortful try—fail—adjust" loop. Without that loop, there is no deliberate practice and no real skill building.

Analogy: the gym. A coach can give feedback on form and load, but if the coach lifts for you, your muscles don't grow. AI can be your coach, but it cannot lift for you.

5.3.4 The AI Tutor Dilemma: Giving Answers vs. Socratic Questioning

When AI acts as a tutor, a central tension appears: giving answers directly solves problems quickly but may undermine learning; Socratic questioning (苏格拉底式追问)—guiding learners to discover through questions—deepens understanding but takes longer and demands more from prompt design.

Dong et al. (2025) propose in "The Cognitive Mirror" (认知之镜): have AI play the "teachable novice" (可被教的初学者) rather than the omniscient oracle. The learner explains concepts to AI; AI acts as a confused student, asking for clarification. This "teaching others" setup forces learners to organize knowledge and detect gaps—the Feynman technique (费曼技巧) digitized.

Metacognitive takeaway: when learning with AI, choose your mode. When you need quick output, asking for answers is fine. When you need real internalization, try having AI play the questioner or novice, forcing you to articulate your understanding.

5.3.5 Thought Experiment: The Bicycle Analogy

AI is like an electric bicycle. It gets you there faster with less effort. But if you never pedal, your legs atrophy. When the battery dies—novel problems, missing data, unprecedented situations—can you still ride?

Many knowledge workers are living "the electric bicycle effect": they use AI for routine tasks, efficiently. But when they hit problems AI can't answer, when they must decide on incomplete signals, when they need to create solutions that have never existed, they find they "can't ride anymore." They depend on AI's output without building the ability to ride on their own.

The metacognitive question: Are you using AI to amplify your riding, or to replace riding itself? The first is leverage; the second is a crutch. Crutches work on smooth roads; they fail where there is no road.

5.3.6 The Self-Regulated Learning Cycle: Zimmerman's Model

Zimmerman (2002) describes self-regulated learning (自我调节学习) in three phases: forethought (预思)—setting goals, planning, activating motivation; performance (执行)—carrying out learning, monitoring progress, applying strategies; self-reflection (反思)—evaluating results, attribution, adjusting next steps.

AI can support each phase. In forethought, AI helps break down goals and draft plans. In performance, AI offers instant answers, explanations, practice. In reflection, AI helps summarize, compare, and spot weaknesses. But only if the learner keeps metacognitive control. If control shifts entirely to AI—letting it decide what to learn, when, and how well—AI becomes a crutch (拐杖) instead of a scaffold (脚手架). Scaffolds gradually withdraw to foster independence; crutches breed dependence.

5.3.7 When to Use AI as Tutor, When to Struggle Alone: A Decision Framework

Chapter 4 discussed the four quadrants of attention allocation (Creating, Evaluating, Consuming, Reacting). Switching between learning mode and production mode is essentially an attention allocation decision: are you directing attention toward 'producing output' or 'building capability'? This trade-off requires metacognitive awareness.

A practical metacognitive framework rests on three questions:

  1. Is this a skill I need to internalize? If it's part of your core capability and worth holding long-term, investing in struggle pays off. If it's a one-off task or peripheral skill, using AI to speed up is rational.

  2. Am I in learning mode or production mode? In learning mode, the goal is "I can do it"; in production mode, it's "it's done." You can alternate—but stay honest. Don't tell yourself you're learning while always asking for ready answers.

  3. Will AI assistance here build or erode my capability? If AI skips the effortful attempt and gives the answer, you may lose the chance to build mental models. If AI provides feedback, hints, or Socratic questions, it may be building capability.

5.4 Counter-Argument: Not All Struggle Is Productive

Not every difficulty is "desirable." Sometimes the fastest path is the right path.

AI assistance can free cognitive resources for higher-order learning. If you use AI for repetitive tasks—format conversion, data cleaning, template filling—and put the freed capacity toward creative thinking or deep understanding, that's a win. The issue is not "use AI or not" but intentionality (意向性): Are you consciously choosing to take the shortcut, or defaulting to dependence?

Some kinds of "struggle" are pure waste. Spending two hours on a typo, trial-and-error with outdated docs, reinventing the wheel where solutions exist—these are not desirable difficulties but ineffective drain. Metacognition helps you distinguish: which difficulties to keep, which to bypass with tools.

5.5 Metacognitive Moment: Three Reflection Exercises

Exercise 1: The Learning vs. Performance Audit

List your last five uses of AI. Any context—coding, emails, research, analysis.

For each, ask:

  1. Was I in learning mode or production mode? Was my main goal "to learn" or "to finish"?
  2. Did I make this choice consciously? Or did I open AI out of habit?
  3. If I could redo it, would I choose differently? Why?

Many discover they rarely explicitly distinguish (有意识地区分) these modes. They default to AI because it's "faster." The audit makes that default visible so you can reclaim choice.

Exercise 2: The Struggle Budget

Allocate a "struggle budget" for the coming week. Deliberately decide: when will you choose to struggle without AI? When will you use AI to boost output?

Write it down. Example: "Tuesday morning, learning the new framework's API—first two hours, no AI, read docs and try myself." "Wednesday afternoon, project update—use AI for first draft." The ratio doesn't matter—one person might use AI 80%, another 20%—intentionality does. You're allocating by plan, not by habit.

Exercise 3: The Transfer Test

Recall something you "learned with AI" last month—a technical concept, a tool, a problem-solving approach.

Ask: Can I explain it from scratch? No AI, no notes; teach it on paper or whiteboard. If yes, learning happened. Can I apply it in a new context? Switch to a similar but different situation—can you transfer?

If both fail, perhaps you gained performance, not learning. That's not failure—next time, you can deliberately preserve more difficulty and let real internalization happen.

5.6 Summary and Transition

Chapter Takeaways

  • Learning ≠ performance: AI boosts immediate performance; learning requires desirable difficulties and productive struggle.
  • Bloom and AI capability: AI handles Remember/Understand/Apply; humans lead in Evaluate/Create. The metacognitive question: "Do I need to go higher?"
  • Desirable difficulties: Spacing, interleaving, retrieval—making learning harder improves long-term outcomes; AI shortcuts remove difficulty and learning.
  • Deliberate practice: Growth happens at the edge of ability; AI can give feedback but cannot "lift for you."
  • Self-regulated learning: In the forethought—performance—reflection cycle, keep metacognitive control; use AI as scaffold, not crutch.
  • Decision framework: Internalize? Learn or produce? Build or erode?

From "learning to learn" (this chapter) we move to the hardest metacognitive skill: value judgment. Knowing what to learn and how still lands on "what is worth doing" and "what is right"—this is not cognition but ethics and values. Next, we turn to metacognition and value judgment—how humans make choices that are genuinely their own when AI can produce almost everything.
